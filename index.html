What Selling Actually Is

Foundations: the three things, and the engine underneath them.

Right people → Right message → Numbers game
The big idea

All of sales — new business or growing accounts — comes down to three things, in order. Nail the first two and the rest is just a numbers game. The engine under all of it is pain: people don't change on logic, they change when they have an emotionally compelling reason to.

1

The Three Things

Framework
  • Quality of prospect → quality of messaging → numbers game. They go in that order, every time.
  • Quality of prospect: am I talking to the right companies, and the right people inside them? And is my list any good?
  • Quality of messaging: how relevant are the pain points I raise to the person in front of me? (This is the 30-second commercial.)
  • Get the first two right and volume takes care of itself.
2

Pain Is the Engine

Pain
  • The most potent human motivator isn't fear — it's pain. It's the emotionally compelling reason a person changes.
  • They're always making one of two decisions: do something vs. nothing, or choosing between options (us vs. internal vs. another agency).
  • Your job: help them figure out (a) do they even have pain, and (b) is it big enough to act on. Doing nothing is comfortable.
3

Corporate vs. Personal Pain

Pain
  • The same problem hits each person differently. Find the personal version — it's far more potent than company pain.
  • A revenue miss takes money out of the CEO's kids' college fund; for the channel owner it puts their job in danger. Same problem, different stakes.
Say it like this

"And how does that affect you?" — not the company.

4

The Duct-Taped Box

Discovery
  • We all have problems we box up, duct-tape, and shove in a corner of our mind. Nobody can tackle everything.
  • Your job is to help them pull the box out, peel the tape, look at the problem, and decide: do something about it, or stop worrying about it.
5

Budgets Are Speed Limits

Budget
  • That 15-mph sign on the corner? You do 25. A budget is a sign, not a wall.
  • Enough personal pain and they find the money. No pain, no money — no matter the "budget."
6

Build Your Dream-Client List

ICP
  • Don't start from abstract criteria. Start from three current clients you'd love to duplicate.
  • From there we reverse-engineer objective search criteria to find lookalikes (that's the next session).
  • Make the mental shift: from "how do I market this brand" to "what kind of company would want to hire us."

Your Next Moves

  • Write down 3 current clients you'd love to clone — we'll build lookalike criteria from them.
  • On your next call, when you hear a problem, ask "how does that affect you?" to get to the personal pain.
  • Keep pushing for clear revenue targets so we're aiming at something, not just running.

The Discovery Call

Where deals are won or lost — in about ten minutes.

Set the frame → Ask the pivot question → Chase the real pain
The big idea

The discovery call decides everything, and it can take 10 minutes, not an hour. Don't read them a brochure. Set an up-front contract, ask one good pivot question, then get genuinely curious and follow the pain wherever it goes.

1

The Up-Front Contract

Set the frame
  • Three parts: time, agenda, outcome. The most important — and most skipped — is outcome.
  • Outcome = what are we trying to decide here? "Yes means we book a walkthrough. And no is okay — if we can't help, we part ways friends."
  • Saying "no is okay" out loud takes the pressure off, so you stop getting "this was so helpful, can you send me something" followed by silence.
2

The Pivot Question

The opener

After the small talk and the frame, the whole call turns on one question. Almost stutter it, like you're thinking out loud — it's more conversational that way.

Say it like this

"Just so I'm not sitting here reading you a brochure about PartnerCentric — help me understand, what made you interested in scheduling this call? And then I'll try to help any way I can."

Then shut up.

3

The Pain Word Isn't the Problem

Discovery
  • Whatever comes out first is the cream on top of the coffee — not the real thing. Problems sit in our heads like a tangled web of wires.
  • Humans untangle by talking, so ask 2, 3, 4, 8 questions to help them think it through.
  • Be a heat-seeking missile for pain words. "We're having issues with attribution." → "Issues — what do you mean?"
4

Curiosity Is the Ingredient

Discovery
  • Two non-negotiables: be genuinely curious (people feel the spark, even on Zoom 3,000 miles away), and make every question build off what they just said — never a checklist.
  • The moment it feels like leading questions or "sales mode," they feel it and pull back. You know the feeling.
5

Read Them in 30 Seconds

DISC
  • You don't need their full profile. Two quick reads: task- or people-oriented? Introvert or extrovert? That's enough to adapt.
  • Match and mirror their pace and depth. The hardest people to sell are the ones unlike you — adapt to them, not the other way around.
6

Questions Beat Statements

Credibility
  • Anything you can say as a statement, you can ask as a question. Ask one they can't answer and your credibility jumps.
The demo

"How did you leverage outside properties to lower your tax return this year?"

You don't know — so you assume whoever asked is the expert.

7

Play Like a 5-Year-Old

Mindset
  • A coach told a team of five-year-olds: kick it two feet ahead, catch up, repeat — and when you're standing in front of the goal, tap it in.
  • Do the early steps (frame, pain, curiosity) really well and the close is the easiest part. Don't sprint to the goal.

Your Next Moves

  • Run the up-front contract on every discovery call — especially the outcome and "no is okay."
  • Memorize the pivot question, practice the stutter, then shut up.
  • Keep a pen on every call and hunt for pain words; follow up off what they actually said, not a script.

Your Prospecting Engine

Quality of prospect comes first. Build the list, then work it.

Right companies → Right people → Right list → Keep going
The big idea

Quality of prospect comes first. Start from the clients you'd clone, build objective lookalike criteria, then let the tools do the heavy lifting. You can't control outcomes — so control your activities and keep selling.

1

The Three Things, In Order

Framework
  • Quality of prospect → quality of messaging → numbers game. Always that order.
  • Nail the first two and volume becomes math, not luck.
2

Then the Right People

Targeting
  • Inside those companies, who's the persona? What pain do they carry relative to what you sell?
  • A small, sharp list of the right titles beats a big sloppy one. A bad list = wasted dials and dead emails.
3

Find Lookalikes the Right Way

ICP
  • Start from three dream clients (Musely was the example), then find what they have in common.
  • Use two reliable, objective filters: employee headcount (not revenue — public revenue data is unreliable) and industry / NAICS codes.
  • Build a search that finds more companies like your best ones.
Watch for

"AI-forward" or "rapidly growing" isn't a filter you can actually search. Headcount + industry is. Turn the vibe into objective criteria.

4

Let the Tools Carry the Weight

Tools
  • ZoomInfo + Claude: pull the list (right industries, size, territory), then have Claude supplement it — persona, most-likely pain points, and best channel to reach them (mobile, email, or both).
  • Chris is a sensei at this. Bring her your criteria and she can build the list.
5

The Cookbook

Numbers game
  • You can't control outcomes — only activities. The Cookbook is the set of actions in your direct control that hit your number.
  • Focus only on those. Everything else takes care of itself.
6

Just Keep Selling

Mindset
  • Worried a full portfolio means you can't take more? The fix: don't stare at your calendar — keep selling.
  • Some clients always fall off. Stop filling the top and you'll be scrambling later.

Your Next Moves

  • Write your 3 dream clients and pull their headcount + industry — that's your first search filter.
  • Get with Chris (and ZoomInfo / Apollo) to build a lookalike list.
  • Pick your Cookbook numbers: the weekly activities you actually control.

Let Pain Do the Selling

Stop convincing. Especially on a new, hard-to-measure service.

Drop the convincing → Surface the pain → Qualify before you solve
The big idea

If it feels like snake oil, that's the tell — you're convincing instead of uncovering. Nobody wants to be "salesy" anymore, and that's good news: the easy, non-salesy move is to get them talking about the problem first. Doubly true for a new service like AI Discovery.

1

"It Feels Like Snake Oil" Is a Signal

Mindset
  • That discomfort means you're in convince-mode — pitching a solution before there's a felt problem.
  • Today nobody raises their hand to be "salesy," and the one who does is usually the salesiest in the room. The non-salesy move is also the easier one: ask, don't pitch.
  • Once you see it, the things you thought you were "supposed" to do are the salesy things. Do the opposite and it gets lighter.
2

Get Pain Started ASAP

Pain
  • People buy on emotion, then justify with logic. Your first job isn't "are they interested in AI?" — it's "do they have a problem we solve, and does it matter enough?"
Open with

"What made you show up today?"

If they hand you a real problem, great. If not, give them a menu.

3

The #1 AI Objection

Objections
  • For AI Discovery, the real blocker is tie-back-to-revenue: "how do we measure this and prove it's worth it?"
  • Don't fake a perfect number. Anchor to what's knowable: current AI/LLM-referred traffic and conversion, then frame the upside range. Honesty about the range builds trust.
4

The Pain Menu

Discovery

When they say "I just want to see what you've got," don't launch into what you do. Offer two third-party scenarios and let them pick the one that fits.

Say it like this

"Let me share a couple things we hear — tell me which is most relevant. A lot of people, before they worked with us, said either: 'we hear our competitors are doing things with AI and we're nervous we're behind,' or 'we can't tie any of this back to revenue, so we won't commit budget.' Either of those land?"

Then don't solve. Go curious: "Really? What do you mean? Give me an example. How long's that been going on? What happens if you don't fix it?"

5

Fish-Hook the AI Pain

Third-party stories
  • On an affiliate-led call where AI never comes up, dangle it as a third-party story rather than a pivot to pitch.
The hook

"We've noticed a lot of folks like you trying to figure out AI but unsure how to tie it to revenue — you running into that at all?"

6

Don't Take the Bait to Solve

Discipline
  • They'll push: "OK, but how do you get me from 10 to 40, and what's the revenue?" That's the trap.
  • Get them talking about the problem and confirm it matters before solve-mode. Otherwise you present big, they do nothing, and you've burned the call again.

Your Next Moves

  • Write your two best third-party pain scenarios for AI Discovery in plain, 7th-grade language.
  • Open every call with "what made you show up?" and resist solve-mode until the pain is real and it matters.
  • Build a simple "current AI-referred revenue" baseline question into discovery so the value talk has a number.

The Full Map

The entire system in one picture — and the seven steps that flip it.

The buyer's system beats yours → Flip it with seven steps
The big idea

Left to default, the buyer's system beats the seller's system 100% of the time. The whole method is one move: stop playing the seller's game (pitch → propose → chase) and run a different process built on rapport, an up-front contract, and real information-gathering before you ever present.

1

Seller System vs. Buyer System

The problem
  • The standard sell: generate interest → discover → present → price → follow up. Almost everything you'll ever sell follows those five steps.
  • The trouble: put that next to how buyers actually behave, and the buyer's system wins every time. It's just stronger.
2

The Cabinet Store

How buyers really act

Dan's mother-in-law redoing a kitchen — the four moves every buyer makes:

  • Lie — "I'm just looking." (She's not; she knows exactly what she wants.)
  • Steal — collects your ideas, pricing, and time to shop around or keep a current vendor honest.
  • Positive lie — "You've been so helpful!" Karma padding right before she ghosts.
  • Hide — the "following up" email goes unanswered. If she does answer: "can you send an updated proposal?" and it starts over.
Sound familiar?

Three quotes, all your ideas, hires the cheap guy. That's the 86 "follow up" tasks sitting in your CRM right now.

3

Steps 1–2: Set the Stage

Bond & frame
  • Bond & build rapport: match and mirror — fast/slow, detail/big-picture, all-business/personal. Adapt to them.
  • Up-front contract: time, agenda, outcome. Outcome is king, and "no is okay" keeps the conversation honest.
4

Step 6: Present Back

Presenting
  • Only now do you present — and only back to what you found: the problem you're solving and what matters to each person in the room.
  • No generic deck. You're answering their pain, not performing.
5

Steps 3–5: Gather Information

The middle

This is where you differ from everyone else — you gather, you don't pitch.

  • Pain (the holy grail, all through questions): are they do-something-vs-nothing, or choosing between options? Ask about the consequences of doing nothing, or the cost of the wrong choice.
  • Decision: process (how they decide) + criteria (what matters to each person). The silent killer of deals — almost everyone skips it.
  • Budget: money pain changes how people see money. Get budget (or price-flinch them) before you present.
6

Step 7: Post-Sell

Don't forget
  • Post-sell is a verb: defuse future bombs before they go off.
  • Window-tint story: "It'll bubble up Monday — totally normal." Because he warned me, I wasn't mad. Name what could go wrong before it does.
Olivia's real question

On a brand-new service, how do you give enough that they feel they got value — without dumping everything? That tension is exactly what Session 6 answers.

Your Next Moves

  • Google "Sandler Submarine" (or Flywheel — same thing) and keep the seven steps visible on your desk.
  • Before your next present, write the two things you'll present back to: their pain and each person's decision criteria.
  • Add one post-sell move to your next proposal — name a future bomb and defuse it up front.

Start Small

Sell the small first step. Let pain sell the rest.

Start small → Let pain sell → Present back → Follow up human
The big idea

Stop selling the whole scope up front. Sell a small, paid first step, uncover real pain, and present back only what they care about. When they get overwhelmed by everything, they retreat to the easiest thing. Give them the easy yes on purpose.

1

The Monkey's Paw

Start small
  • A small thing that pulls a bigger thing up. The knot goes over the ship first, then hauls the heavy line behind it.
  • In sales, that small thing is the Discovery and Audit. Sell that first, not the full scope.
  • It builds trust, gets you in their system as an approved vendor, and locks in real commitment because they actually pay for it.
  • It should stand on its own and deliver value by itself.
  • Think coffee before the engagement ring. Nobody proposes to a stranger.
Do the math

Price the first step at roughly 10% of the annual full-scope value. Full scope at $7,500/month is about $90K/year, so the Discovery and Audit lands around $9K, one time.

2

Pain Does the Selling

Discovery
  • Pain is the most potent human motivator. People buy when the pain outweighs the cost and the hassle.
  • You cannot force it. Your only job is to get them talking about it.
  • Be okay if they reach the end and realize it is not a real problem. That is useful information, not a loss.
Watch for

"Can you send me something" means they are punting. You skipped pain or did not dig deep enough. If a kid's leg were broken, nobody asks for a brochure. They ask where to sign.

3

The Pain Menu

Discovery
  • When they say "I just want to see what you've got," do not launch into what you do.
  • Hand them a menu of pain points and let them pick the one that fits.
Say it like this

"Would it help if I shared what other people tell us they are working on? You tell me which one is most relevant."

Then name two common pains those clients have. Keep it in plain, 7th-grade language a 12 year old could repeat.

4

Right Person, Right Company

Targeting
  • If the right person at the right company agrees to meet, there is a pain point. It does not matter how much they already know about you.
  • This is why outbound matters. Inbound leads chasing a shiny new service often do not care enough to pay for it.
  • Get in front of your ICP first, then hand them the pain menu.
5

The Pre-Call Plan

Prep
  • What questions do I need to ask?
  • What is my ideal outcome for this call?
  • What might they ask me, and how will I handle it?
  • What am I nervous about?

Run this before every call. Then present back only to your answers. Template is on its way from Dan.

6

Say It Gently. Pat It First.

Communication
  • Anytime you are nervous to say something, pat it before you say it.
  • Then say the real thing. You already do this in your personal life. Same muscle.
Say it like this

"Can I say something? Promise you won't get mad." or "I've got a random thought, can I run it by you?"

Example: "I'm a little nervous there's an affiliate angle that fits, but I think you might throw the rest out."

7

The Starbucks Call

Follow-up
  • The informal follow-up. Picture being in line at Starbucks, both of you rushed.
  • Not a sweaty "just checking in" call from behind your desk. One quick question, out of the blue.
  • It is refreshing when your intent is genuine. Most people never do it.
Say it like this

"Hey, quick question for you. How do you think that call went?"

8

Run the Room

Presenting

Highest title is not the leader

  • The senior person may not know the details. Do not let the call default to them.
  • With a full room, that is the moment to present back what you uncovered. It is not the moment to discover from scratch.
  • If it gets murky or people talk over each other, sidebar one decision-maker afterward with a Starbucks call.

Do not out-polish the moment

  • A perfectly scripted deck, slide 2, slide 3, slide 4, lands worse than going live off what is happening now.
  • Pull up a whiteboard. Create the aha in real time.
Live demo move

"Pull up Perplexity. Search cribs. Do you show up? No? Does that matter?"

Your Next Moves

  • Run the Pre-Call Plan before your next calls. Dan is sending the template.
  • Define the two most potent pain points for the AI Discovery service. Plain language a 12 year old could say out loud.
  • On Million Dollar Baby: make the informal follow-up call, lead it, and surface the affiliate angle gently.
  • Lock the handoff. Discovery and warm-up first, then Olivia in for technical requirements and present-back.
